Are politicians lying more today than 10 years ago?

The answer is yes according to fact-checking website data

President Donald Trump meets with Finnish President Sauli Niinistö Oct. 2 (The White House/Flickr)

By Amy O'Kruk

Published September, 2019

In 2016, it was post-truth. In 2017, fake news. Last year, misinformation took the top spot.

All three terms have been crowned “word of the year” by the Oxford Dictionary and other reference books or sites. Together, they reflect a world where some feel politicians shape public opinion by appealing to feelings rather than facts by any means necessary.

But is the flood of fake news actually worse today than in the past?

The answer appears to be yes. With the deluge of online misinformation has come a surge in fact-checking efforts. Since 2007, reporters and editors at Politifact.com have evaluated the accuracy of over 16,500 statements made by politicians, candidates and others, assigning scores ranging from "True" for completely accurate statements to "Pants on Fire" for ridiculous claims.

Over the last decade, the number of bogus statements has shot up. In 2018, 41 percent of statements were considered false or ridiculous, compared to 26 percent in 2008. At the same time, the percentage of true statements has nosedived.

The upturn in fabrications gained momentum in 2015, a time when voters were especially bombarded by misinformation in the lead up to the 2016 U.S. presidential election. In the weeks and months afterwards, many politicians and pundits said they feared that fake news and conspiracy articles spread on social media had swayed the election results.

U.S. President Donald Trump is a prolific source of fishy claims. Before and after his election, Politifact has stamped the majority of his statements as mostly false, false or wildly inaccurate. In his two years as president, he’s been fact checked more than than the eight years of his predecessor, Barack Obama. Politifact states that it’s nonpartisan and selects claims to vet based on their newsworthiness and significance.

Other fact checkers have reported similar findings. In August, the Washington Post reported Trump has made over 12,000 false or misleading statements, often repeating debunked claims, especially on topics such as jobs, immigration and forgein policy.

But if politicians are more mistruthful these days, what about pundits? Politifact launched a sister website in 2013 called Punditfact, which checks claims by media figures, columnists, bloggers and talk-show hosts and guests.

Since its founding, ratings for pundits’ statements have stayed more consistent, although there was a striking jump in the number of statements rated ridiculous between 2016 to 2018.

This was largely due to an influx of bloggers. In 2017, over 40 percent of Punditfact’s fact-checks were from blogs. This umbrella term often includes hyper-partisan websites or well-known sources of fake news. such as TheLastLineOfDefense.org and PuppetStringNews.com. These claims are overwhelmingly unreliable; statements from blogs score a "Pants on Fire!" rating 64 percent of the time. For example, Politifact recently debunked claims from bloggers and Facebook users that asserted sex trafficker Jeffrey Epstein is still alive.

In the wake of ongoing criticism, Facebook and other social media companies have pledged to make a range of algorithmic and policy changes to try and limit the spread of false content.

But with the 2020 presidential election on the horizon, voters may want to be wary of where they get their information and do a little fact checking themselves.

Data and methods:

This analysis is based on Politifact's fact checks scored True, Mostly True, Half True, Mostly False, False and Pants on Fire. Occasionally, Politifact scored a statement as a Full Flop, Half Flip or No Flip. I removed these entries from my analysis due to their inconsistent and infrequent use.

I collected the data by web scraping PolitiFact.com's published fact checks from May 2, 2007 to Sept 19, 2019. I followed the same procedure for PunditFact.com, although the website was launched later in November 2013.

Some years, Politifact fact checked more statements than others. As a result, I based my analysis on the "Truth-O-Meter" ratings' percentage frequency rather than total counts.
