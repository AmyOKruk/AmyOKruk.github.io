Another week, another massive corporate security breach that endangers your personal data

These incidents are often invisible -- until they aren’t

By Amy O'Kruk

Published September 15, 2019

Editor's note: Much of the content here is taken from Information is Beautiful and its "World's Biggest Data Breaches & Hacks" project. This article is an excercise in how to transform a "data dump" into a narrative story (and hey, it's responsive!).

With nine months of 2019 on the books, it's been a good year for cyber bandits.

Data breaches have doubled this year over last, according to a Baltimore IT security firm. In July, news broke that a hacker had stolen the personal information of millions of Capital One credit card customers. The hacker, 33-year-old Seattle native Paige Thompson, downloaded terabytes of Social Security numbers, bank account and credit card information to peddle on the dark web. But even with 100 million Americans and 6 million Canadians affected, the Capital One hack doesn't crack the top 10 data breaches of all time.

Attacks are often underestimated, missed or downplayed

While we're used to hearing about hacks, we rarely see the fallout.

Sometimes, it's a waiting game. Hackers who steal login credentials, for example, may use them for under-the-radar crime sprees instead of selling or publishing the data. As a result, the repercussions of a breach can be postponed, sometimes not fully manifesting for years.

It can also take years for companies to admit a breach.

In 2013, digital thieves plundered Yahoo and made off with names, birth dates, phone numbers and passwords of users that were encrypted with security that was easy to crack.

First Yahoo said 500 million users were affected. Then one billion. Finally, four years after the fact, the company admits all three billion of its user accounts were compromised, crowning it the biggest data breach of all time.

No one knows exactly what happened to the data after it was pinched. However, last August, a hacking collective based in Eastern Europe quietly began touting the Yahoo information for sale, according to the New York Times.

When it comes to data exposures, hackers are the greatest threat

Not all incidents are caused by hackers, but most are. Data “exposures” occur when information that should have been locked down was accessible, but it’s unclear if anyone actually stole it. For example, in cases such as poor security, lost computers or accidental publishing.
