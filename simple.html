What do we talk about when we talk to aliens?

In 2009, people from around the world sent thousands of messages to Gliese 581d, a "super Earth" 20 light-years away. These messages reveal insights into our feelings and concerns about our planet and life beyond it

By Amy O'Kruk

Published August, 2019

Are we alone in the universe? Are there civilizations on other planets? When aliens look up at the stars, do they feel small and lonely just like us?

These were some of the questions posed in 2009, when Australia’s COSMOS magazine launched Hello From Earth. The project, in celebration of the country’s National Science Week, collected messages from people around the world to send to the closest Earth-like planet outside our solar system, Gliese 581d.

Gliese 581d is about eight times heavier than Earth and orbits a low-mass red dwarf star in the constellation Libra. The planet has been called the “first serious waterworld candidate,” according to Stephane Udry, an astronomer from the Geneva Observatory in Switzerland who helped discover the planet in 2007.

Restricting the messages to 160 characters and the English language, people across continents had a lot to say to the planet's potential inhabitants, entering over 20,000 submissions. The messages were transmitted to Gliese 581d from NASA's Canberra DeepSpace Communication Complex at Tidbinbilla.

These messages offer a look at the constellation of feelings and concerns that dominate our thoughts when we think about aliens and the search for other intelligent life.

Humanity is friendly

Overwhelmingly, people expressed messages of peace, curiosity or even anxiety for the future.

To find out what we talked about the most, I randomly sampled the submissions and identified themes; for example, messages of peace. I used these samples to create lists of key words. In the case of peaceful messages, they likely include terms such as “peace, hope, prosperity, love, enjoy, friends.”

Hello From Earth wasn’t the first Search for Extra-Terrestrial Intelligence (SETI) project, and it won’t be the last. Since then other radio signals have been sent by organizers in Europe and the United States. Humans continues to toss bottles into the cosmic ocean, so to speak.

Will we hear anything back? Who knows. The Hello From Earth messages are still on their way to their final destination. They'll reach the exoplanet in December 2029 and it would take at least another 10 years for us to receive a reply signal.

As the search for extraterrestrial life continues, one thing is clear, humans are curious: We want to know what — or who — is out there.

Data and method

For this project, I collected the Hello From Earth messages using the Internet Archive’s Wayback Machine. Previously, the project’s website, hellofromearth.net, posted user submissions.

One limitation of this analysis was that the Wayback Machine’s archive of hellofromearth.net was incomplete. Out of over 20,000 submissions, I was able to scrape 10,600. Although incomplete, I felt this was still a reasonable sample size to work with and draw some conclusions from. (And hey, it’s also a fun personal project!)

To visualize the messages, I vectorized them using the encoding approach TF–IDF, finding each messages most characteristic terms. I used a data science technique called t-SNE to cluster similar messages together and derive the constellation of feelings. Messages were color coded according to if they contained key words or phrases associated with each sentiment (peace, are you out there?, come visit and climate change).

As for the demographics of respondents, there’s wasn’t much to go on: Participants needed only to submit a message, name and location. Some groups, such as Australians, are certainly overrepresented because the project originated in Australia. Nonetheless, similar messages and sentiments seemed to be expressed across continents — enough to give readers a sense of broad trends.
